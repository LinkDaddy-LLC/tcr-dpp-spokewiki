ESPR Product Categories: Which Products Need a Digital Product Passport?

The European Union's Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) represents a landmark legislative effort to transform the sustainability of products placed on the EU market. Enacted on July 18, 2024, the ESPR expands significantly upon the previous Ecodesign Directive (2009/125/EC), moving beyond energy-related products to encompass nearly all physical goods. A cornerstone of this regulation is the Digital Product Passport (DPP), a digital record designed to enhance transparency, traceability, and circularity throughout a product's lifecycle. Understanding which product categories are in scope, and when, is critical for manufacturers, importers, and distributors worldwide, as the regulation impacts any entity selling into the vast EU market, regardless of their country of origin.

The implementation of the ESPR is not a monolithic event but a phased rollout, primarily governed by a mechanism known as delegated acts. These acts are product-specific legal instruments issued by the European Commission that translate the broad framework of the ESPR into concrete, enforceable rules for individual product categories. They define specific performance parameters—such as durability, repairability, recyclability, carbon footprint, and chemical content—and crucially, specify the data that must be included in a product's Digital Product Passport. This dynamic and evolving regulatory landscape necessitates a proactive approach from businesses to ensure compliance and maintain market access.

The ESPR Delegated Act Mechanism: How Product Categories are Selected

The ESPR itself, Regulation (EU) 2024/1781, provides the overarching legal basis, governance structures, and fundamental principles for sustainable products. However, the granular details—the "what" and "how" of compliance for specific products—are elaborated through delegated acts. This legislative architecture allows the European Commission to adapt and introduce specific requirements for various product groups without undergoing the full, lengthy legislative process for each category. This makes the delegated acts powerful and relatively fast-moving instruments for regulatory change [1].

Each delegated act focuses on a particular product group and defines several key aspects:

  • Performance Parameters: Which sustainability aspects apply (e.g., durability, repairability, recyclability, carbon footprint, chemical content).
  • Minimum Threshold Values: The specific standards manufacturers must meet for these parameters.
  • Information Requirements: Crucially, what data must be made available through the Digital Product Passport.
  • Conformity Assessment Procedures: How compliance will be verified.
  • Transition Timelines: Typically, an 18 to 36-month period after publication before the act becomes legally binding [2].

Once a delegated act enters into force, it has direct legal effect across all 27 EU member states, eliminating the need for additional national transposition. The selection of product categories for these delegated acts is guided by several factors, including their potential for environmental impact reduction, market readiness, sales volume, and stakeholder support. The EU Joint Research Centre (JRC) plays a significant role in identifying high-impact categories through preparatory studies and recommendations [3].

The Commission adopted the first ESPR Working Plan (COM(2025) 187) on April 16, 2025, outlining a five-year roadmap for delegated act development. This plan identifies priority product groups and horizontal requirements, providing businesses with an indicative timeline for upcoming obligations [2].

The Confirmed First Wave: Textiles, Batteries, Electronics/ICT, Construction Products, Furniture

The initial phase of ESPR implementation focuses on product categories identified as having significant environmental impact and market readiness for sustainable transformation. The first ESPR Working Plan 2025-2030, adopted on April 16, 2025, outlines these priority groups. It's important to note that while some categories are directly under ESPR, others, like batteries, are governed by complementary regulations [2].

Textiles and Apparel

Textiles and apparel are a top priority due to their substantial environmental footprint, complex global supply chains, and significant waste generation. The delegated act for textiles is targeted for adoption in 2027, with a preparatory study completed in 2024. Key requirements are expected to include minimum recycled fiber content, durability benchmarks, and comprehensive textile product passports detailing fiber composition, chemical usage, and end-of-life instructions. Footwear is currently undergoing a separate study, with its inclusion pending future assessment [2] [4].

Batteries

Batteries are not directly part of the ESPR Working Plan but are covered by the separate EU Battery Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2023/1542). This regulation already mandates a battery passport for industrial and electric vehicle (EV) batteries above 2 kWh from February 2027. The Battery Regulation sets extensive requirements, including information on battery chemistry, carbon footprint per kWh, recycled content percentages for critical raw materials (cobalt, lithium, nickel, lead), due diligence on critical raw materials, and dynamic State of Health data. The ESPR framework may extend requirements to other battery categories not covered by this specific regulation in future working plans [2] [5].

Electronics and ICT Products

While not explicitly listed as a product-specific priority in the first wave, Information and Communication Technology (ICT) products are a significant focus through horizontal requirements and related work on energy-related products. The ESPR 2025-2030 working plan includes the development of horizontal measures such as repairability and recyclability for electrical and electronic equipment. Specific ICT products, like displays and EV chargers, are also addressed under energy-related product regulations with adoption dates ranging from 2026 to 2028. The DPP for ICT and electronics is likely to require information on critical raw materials, energy efficiency, repairability, lifetime, update policies, and disassembly instructions. The delegated act is expected in 2026-2027, with DPP requirements around 2028-2029 [1] [2] [5].

Construction Products

Construction products follow a distinct regulatory pathway through the revised Construction Products Regulation (EU 2024/3110), applicable since January 8, 2026. The CPR Working Plan 2026-2029 provides an indicative roadmap for various product families. Environmental reporting, including Global Warming Potential (GWP) declarations, is being phased in, with core environmental indicators by 2030 and full life cycle indicators by 2032. The CPR also mandates a Declaration of Performance and Conformity (DoPC) in a structured, machine-readable format. In cases of conflict, the CPR takes precedence over the ESPR for construction products [5].

Furniture and Mattresses

Furniture and mattresses are grouped together in the Working Plan, with delegated acts expected in the 2027-2028 window. The focus for these categories includes recycled and bio-based material content, durability claims, disassembly instructions, and disclosure of chemical substances. Mattresses, specifically, will be regulated to enhance material efficiency, reduce waste, and extend product longevity, with a delegated act expected in 2029 and DPP obligations around 2030-2031 [1] [2] [5].

The Second Wave: Tyres, Detergents, Paints, Steel/Aluminium

Beyond the immediate priorities, the ESPR Working Plan 2025-2030 also identifies a second wave of product categories and horizontal measures that will be subject to delegated acts and DPP requirements. This phased approach allows for preparatory studies, stakeholder consultations, and the development of robust technical standards [2].

Tyres

Tyres are included in the first Working Plan as a priority final product group, with a delegated act targeted for 2027 and compliance expected by 2029. Despite existing regulations, new ESPR rules will aim to improve recyclability and recycled content, while addressing challenges in end-of-life waste management. Expected requirements include durability benchmarks, rolling resistance thresholds, abrasion rates, and material composition disclosure. Tyre wear is also a significant source of microplastic pollution, which the regulation seeks to mitigate [1] [2] [5].

Steel and Aluminium (Intermediate Products)

Iron and steel are the first intermediate products targeted for a delegated act under the ESPR, with indicative adoption in 2026 and compliance expected by 2028. This prioritization reflects the sector's enormous carbon footprint. The delegated act is expected to focus on embodied carbon disclosure, recycled content thresholds, and information on substances of concern. For steel producers and importers, this necessitates building data infrastructure to track carbon intensity and recycled content ratios across the supply chain [1] [2] [5].

Aluminium joins iron and steel as a priority intermediate product, with indicative adoption in 2027 and compliance expected by 2029. Primary aluminium production is energy-intensive, but secondary (recycled) aluminium uses significantly less energy. The delegated act will likely focus on recycled content verification, embodied carbon per kilogram, and traceability through the value chain [1] [2] [5].

Detergents, Paints, and Lubricants

While not part of the initial priority list in the first Working Plan, detergents, paints, and lubricants are identified in the EU Joint Research Centre (JRC) recommendations as categories with high potential for future ESPR action. The Commission has scheduled a scoping study on chemicals by the end of 2025, which will inform future plans for these product groups. Manufacturers in these sectors should not assume permanent exclusion and should monitor regulatory developments closely [3] [4].

The Working Plan 2025–2030 and Which Categories Are Next

The ESPR First Working Plan 2025-2030 (COM(2025) 187) is a crucial document that maps the five-year roadmap for delegated act development. It outlines which product groups the Commission will regulate first, what horizontal requirements it will introduce, and when businesses should expect binding obligations. A mid-term review is scheduled for 2028, at which point the Commission may add new product groups or adjust timelines based on preparatory study outcomes and stakeholder feedback [2].

Beyond product-specific acts, the plan introduces horizontal measures that cut across categories. These are particularly significant because they apply broadly rather than to a single sector. Examples include a repairability score framework, recyclability requirements for electrical and electronic equipment, and minimum recycled content rules. These horizontal acts will impact a wide range of products, even those not explicitly listed as priority categories [2] [4].

The plan also maintains continuity with 14 energy-related products carried over from the previous Ecodesign Directive (2009/125/EC). These products, including lighting, motors, heaters, and displays, remain under existing measures during a transition period ending December 31, 2026, after which they will be governed by the ESPR framework. The Commission may adopt updated delegated acts with expanded requirements, including Digital Product Passport obligations, for these products [2].

The JRC has recommended 18 high-impact categories for early action, a list that also includes paints and varnishes, detergents, and lubricants, which are important for building product supply chains. This indicates that while not in the immediate first wave, these categories are on the Commission's radar for future regulation [3].

How to Determine if Your Product is in Scope

Given the phased rollout and the product-specific nature of delegated acts, determining whether your product falls within the scope of the ESPR and its DPP requirements can be complex. However, a structured approach can help businesses navigate this evolving regulatory landscape [5].

The general principle is that the ESPR applies to almost all physical products placed on the EU market or put into service, including imports and online goods. Exceptions are made for food, feed, medicine, medical products, and certain vehicles, as these are typically covered by other specific regulations or ecodesign criteria are impractical [4].

To determine if your product is in scope, consider the following steps:

  1. Identify the Product Category: Determine which ESPR product group your product falls into. This involves understanding the classifications used by the European Commission in its working plans and preparatory studies.
  2. Review Annex III of the ESPR: The ESPR (EU 2024/1781) describes in Article 9 and Annex III the broad categories of information to be made available through the DPP. These are the basic requirements that apply to all products in scope.
  3. Consult Delegated Acts: Once published, delegated acts provide the specific, detailed requirements for each product category. These acts define the performance parameters, minimum thresholds, and the exact data fields required for the DPP. Track the preparatory studies and stakeholder consultations for your product categories, as these often signal upcoming requirements with reasonable accuracy [2].
  4. Consider Horizontal Requirements: Be aware of horizontal measures, such as repairability scoring or recyclability requirements, which apply across multiple product groups. These can impact products even if they are not a primary focus of a product-specific delegated act [2].
  5. Assess Your Role in the Supply Chain: Obligations under the ESPR apply to all economic operators, including manufacturers (even those outside the EU), importers, distributors, dealers, and service providers. Your specific role will determine certain additional information requirements, such as importer details and EORI numbers [1] [5].

It is crucial not to wait for the official publication date of a delegated act. Preparatory studies and draft texts circulate for 12 to 24 months before adoption, providing ample opportunity to prepare. Engaging in stakeholder consultations and auditing your current data availability are proactive steps that can prevent last-minute scrambling [2].

The DPP Data Fields Required Per Category: A Differentiated Approach

The Digital Product Passport (DPP) is a central component of the ESPR, designed to provide transparent, traceable, and accessible information about a product's sustainability and circularity. While the ESPR framework establishes the general concept of the DPP, each delegated act specifies the precise data model for its respective product category. This means that the exact data fields required will differ significantly across product groups, necessitating a flexible and adaptable data infrastructure for businesses [2] [5].

The ESPR (EU 2024/1781) outlines broad categories of information in Article 9 and Annex III that must be made available through the DPP. These foundational themes include [5]:

  • Product Identifier: A unique product identifier (UPI), GTIN or equivalent item code, commodity code, facility identifier, and a link to any previous DPPs.
  • Economic Operators: Details of the manufacturer, importer (including EORI number), and, if necessary, an EU responsible representative.
  • Compliance Information: The EU Declaration of Conformity, technical documentation, digital user manual, and safety information.
  • Sustainability and Circularity: Material composition, carbon footprint, expected lifespan, and more.
  • System Requirements and Conditional Fields: Requirements for the DPP itself, such as hosting, availability time, access rights, data integrity, interoperability with the EU DPP registry, and the use of open, machine-readable data formats.

Beyond these basic requirements, delegated acts introduce additional, category-specific information. The emphasis is on "fit-for-purpose" information—data that provides real value to its intended users, from consumers to recyclers [5].

Examples of Category-Specific Data Fields:

  • Batteries: Battery chemistry, carbon footprint per kWh, recycled content percentages for critical raw materials (cobalt, lithium, nickel, lead), State of Health (dynamic data), and due diligence on critical raw materials [5].
  • ICT and Electronics: Critical raw materials, energy efficiency, repairability scores, lifetime, update policies, and disassembly instructions. Software updates are explicitly recognized for their role in product longevity [5].
  • Textiles and Clothing: Fiber composition (type and percentage), washing requirements, microplastics release, PFAS content, and fiber origin [5].
  • Steel and Aluminium: CO₂ emissions per ton, recycled content percentage, alloy composition, and origin of raw materials. These data points often overlap with Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) obligations [5].
  • Tyres: Rolling resistance, wear indicator, noise rating, wet grip, and microplastics wear [5].
  • Furniture: Wood origin and certification (FSC/PEFC), formaldehyde emissions, and disassembly capabilities [5].
  • Mattresses: Flame retardants, VOC emissions, and foam recyclability [5].

Manufacturers must be prepared to collect data on 16 product aspects covering the full life cycle, including durability, reliability, upgradability, repairability, presence of substances of concern, energy and resource efficiency, recycled content, remanufacturing, recyclability, recovery of materials, environmental and carbon footprint, reusability, and expected generation of waste. Each aspect comes with measurable parameters, such as guaranteed lifetime, mean time between failures, spare-parts availability, modularity, ease of disassembly, and microplastic release [6].

A critical aspect is the tracking of Substances of Concern (SoC). The ESPR defines SoC broadly, including substances on the REACH Candidate List (SVHC), those falling under specified CLP hazard classes, regulated Persistent Organic Pollutants, and substances negatively affecting reuse and recycling. This is a horizontal minimum information requirement, meaning every manufacturer in scope of a delegated act will need to disclose SoC data regardless of product group. For each SoC, manufacturers must track IUPAC, EC, and CAS names and numbers, location within the product, concentration, safe-use instructions, and end-of-life information [6].

The DPP also requires a differentiated approach regarding batch versus individual passports. For standardized mass products, a batch DPP (one data set for a series of identical products) may suffice. More complex products with variable components, long lifespans, or relevant use/maintenance data (e.g., electric vehicles) will require a unique DPP per individual product. A hybrid model, combining batch-level basis with product-specific information, is also emerging [5].

Global Impact: Affects Any Exporter Selling into the EU Market Regardless of Country of Origin

The European Union is one of the world's largest and most influential economic blocs. Consequently, regulations enacted within the EU often have a profound global impact, extending far beyond its geographical borders. The Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) and its accompanying Digital Product Passport (DPP) requirements are no exception. This legislation is designed to affect any product placed on the EU market, irrespective of its country of origin, thereby setting a new de facto global standard for product sustainability and transparency [4].

For manufacturers, importers, and distributors located outside the EU, this means that continued access to the lucrative EU market hinges directly on compliance with ESPR. The regulation adheres to international trade principles, and the EU is actively engaging with non-EU countries committed to improving product sustainability. However, the onus is on the economic operator placing the product on the EU market to ensure full compliance [1].

Two key implications highlight the global reach of ESPR and DPP:

  1. Automated Customs and Market Surveillance: The DPP mechanism enables more automated customs checks for imported products. This includes verifying the existence and authenticity of a DPP. Non-compliance is not theoretical; failure to comply can lead to products being blocked from entering the EU, with potential penalties including fines (possibly based on a percentage of company turnover) and market access restrictions [4]. Once the EU DPP Registry goes live (expected July 19, 2026), it will verify formal elements like existence, authenticity, and integrity of the passport, making compliance a market access gate [4].
  2. Supply Chain Transformation: The data requirements for DPPs necessitate deep transparency throughout the entire supply chain. Much of the information required—such as material origins, carbon footprint figures, and substance information—must be sourced from upstream suppliers, often located globally. This compels non-EU suppliers to adopt similar data collection and management practices to support their EU-bound customers. Brands that prepare early by mapping their supply chain data, aligning suppliers, and building compliant product records will gain a significant market access advantage [2] [4].

The ESPR is not merely another reporting initiative; it is fundamentally reshaping how products are designed, manufactured, and traded globally. It forces organizations to maintain verifiable product data continuously through Bill of Materials (BOM) changes, material substitutions, supplier updates, and manufacturing realities. This structural challenge requires a shift towards robust product data operating models, where a single, governed source of product information can serve compliance, sales, marketing, customs, and end-of-life partners [4].

Furthermore, the DPP intersects with other wider EU regulations, such as the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) and the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), creating a synergistic regulatory environment that reinforces sustainability goals across various sectors. This integrated approach means that compliance efforts for one regulation may contribute to compliance with others, further solidifying the EU's role in setting global sustainability benchmarks [5].

Regulatory Timeline Checklist: Preparing for ESPR Compliance

Proactive preparation is essential for navigating the ESPR and DPP requirements. Manufacturers and other economic operators should consider the following checklist to ensure timely compliance and continued market access in the EU.

  1. Immediate Actions (Ongoing):
    • Monitor Regulatory Developments: Continuously track the publication of ESPR Working Plans, preparatory studies, and draft delegated acts relevant to your product categories.
    • Engage in Stakeholder Consultations: Participate in industry consultations to influence requirements and gain early insights into upcoming obligations.
    • Audit Current Data Availability: Assess existing data on material composition, supply chain, environmental performance, and end-of-life characteristics. Identify gaps that need to be addressed for future DPP requirements.
    • Map Supply Chain for Data Collection: Identify key suppliers and initiate conversations about data sharing and traceability requirements.
    • Build a Substance of Concern (SoC) Register: Begin tracking all substances of concern at component and spare-part level, paying particular attention to REACH SVHCs above 0.1% by weight.
  2. Short-Term Actions (Within 12-18 Months of Delegated Act Adoption):
    • Implement Data Infrastructure: Select and implement a technology platform (e.g., PIM system) capable of centralizing, governing, and managing product data at product, batch, and item levels.
    • Develop DPP Data Models: Based on draft delegated acts, begin structuring the specific data fields required for your product categories.
    • Establish Data Validation Processes: Ensure mechanisms are in place to verify the accuracy and integrity of collected data.
    • Prepare for Unsold Goods Disclosure: Inventory unsold product flows against the new disclosure format, including CN codes, units, weight, reasons, and waste-treatment routes (for large enterprises, reporting starts in 2026 for FY 2025 data).
  3. Mid-Term Actions (Leading up to Compliance Deadlines):
    • Integrate with DPP Service Providers: Plan for integration with mandatory EU DPP registry (operational by July 19, 2026) and potential third-party DPP service providers.
    • Implement Data Carriers: Ensure products can be equipped with physical data carriers (e.g., QR codes, NFC tags) linked to their unique product identifiers.
    • Train Internal Teams: Educate relevant departments (R&D, production, sustainability, legal, sales) on ESPR requirements and their roles in data collection and management.
    • Conduct Pilot Programs: Run pilot projects for DPP implementation on a subset of products to identify and resolve challenges before full rollout.
